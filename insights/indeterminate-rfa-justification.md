## 🦋 Insight: Indeterminate RFA Justification: A Request for De-Escalation of Care and Tissue-Sparing Approach

I have a solid, hypervascular TR3 nodule that has been completely stable for over a decade. My pathology is a mix: I have one completely benign biopsy, alongside two indeterminate results with an isolated *NRAS* mutation that were flagged as very borderline benign. Because I am paying out-of-pocket and bypass insurance restrictions, I am seeking an expert ultrasound evaluation to confirm that my smooth margins and 11-year stability make me a safe candidate for a tissue-sparing ablation to turn off this vascular engine. I hypothesize these nodules are follicular adenomas or *NIFTP*, indolent follicular neoplasms.

I have one completely benign biopsy, and the third one was very borderline benign. I was also told the third one looked benign but was shifted toward indeterminate because the team was notified of the Bethesda III/*NRAS* status of the second biopsy (which introduces intrinsic bias, despite it being likely influenced by the reactive atypia hypothesis and also my neurodivergent baseline - interestingly, autistic people have been shown to have high oncogene pathway upregulation but lower relative cancer rates in some studies, and isolated NRAS mutations are nonspecific and not high-risk cancer markers like BRAF/TERT, which I’m negative for). I am terrified of general anesthesia and these nodules are causing severe systemic symptoms (I feel hot all the time, irritable, sweaty, crazy anxious, my hands shake) and compressive symptoms (I can feel them when I swallow, it’s very uncomfortable, and it feels hard to breathe with them especially when lying on my back). I will do anything to save my thyroid. I am willing to self-pay, sign a waiver, and have comprehensively educated myself on the risks of RFA versus surgery for these nodules with my research. I feel like I’m constantly panicking and think these are toxic nodules. The Doppler vascularity imbalance with the one nodule being hypervascular whereas the healthy left side shows little blood flow supports this hypothesis. I decline a nuclear scan due to radiation concerns.

Notably, emerging research, such as a meta-analysis by Cho et al. (2025) showed that thermal ablation is a promising alternative treatment for indeterminate thyroid nodules [1](#ref1), and I accept the risks. I do not want to have a traditional hemithyroidectomy, so the alternative would be watching and waiting, and I think it’s best to ablate the tissue rather than waiting to see if it grows larger. This is an important example of the need for future research to improve genomic analysis and biopsy techniques for indeterminate thyroid nodules. Core needle biopsies (CNB) should be recommended more frequently to visualize tissue architecture and cohesion. I firmly believe de-escalation of care is needed in cases of suspected indolent follicular neoplasms to spare tissue and avoid lifelong hormone replacement.
