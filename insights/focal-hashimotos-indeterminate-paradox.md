## 🦋 Insight: Focal Hashimotos and the Indeterminate Paradox

### The Uncertain Experience
I underwent a repeat biopsy on 4/28/26 for my previously described benign thyroid nodules. Unfortunately, this time, both were classified as Bethesda III (Indeterminate), which carries around a 15-20% risk of malignancy. I was initially shocked by the news and decided to pause my research, but then got inspired to use this as a learning journey. My nodules show Hürthle cell (oncocytic) changes.

### The Deeper Systems-Level Analysis
I integrated my sonographic data into this analysis. My nodules had initially been classified as "nontoxic" and I had been told I did not have lymphocytic thyroiditis (Hashimoto's disease). However, my larger nodule (2.7 cm) shows lymphocytes along with Hürthle cell changes. While this can be concerning, this is a classic example of how Hashimoto's presents cytopathologically. I hypothesize I have chronic focal lymphocytic thyroiditis causing a chronic state of hashitoxicosis. I arrived at this hypothesis by analyzing the topology and Doppler flow of my larger nodule. Although my ultrasound reports homogenous echotexture, the larger nodule shows a "fibrotic-looking" pattern of patchy isoechoic tissue with high perinodular and intranodular vascularity. 
This suggests this may be a complex case of seronegative focal lymphocytic thyroiditis. I have classic Hashimoto's symptoms despite having no antibodies (glossitis, mild non-pitting edema, sensitivity to cold, etc.); I also have profoundly high serum thyroglobulin levels as I mention in my README. Although my endocrinologist asserts I do not have hashimotos, I hypothesize this is a clinical paradox that requires a systems-level approach sensitive to neurodivergent phenotypes. This vascular nodule is clearly leaking Tg into my blood, which may be causing transient thyroid hormone leaks not detectable on a standard blood test but large enough to cause debilitating autoimmune symptoms. I also have a high speckled ANA titer (1:1280), which suggests high levels of autoimmune activity. Many autistic people test positive for high ANA, interestingly. I suspect my autistic phenotype is making the sensory experience of these issues unbearable, and it's exciting an immune system with an already excited initial condition/ground state.
