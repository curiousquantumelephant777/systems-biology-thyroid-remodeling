## 🔬 Hypothesis: Environmental Exposure to Compounds in Sugar-Free Gum and Thyroid Nodule Etiology

### Introduction
While the thyroid nodules of this subject were initially analyzed as a static snapshot (Feb 2025 ultrasound), further invesigation revealed photographic evidence of these significantly sized nodules in June 2015. This led to a systems-biology analysis to determine the potential epigenomic or genomic origin, as the etiology of thyroid nodules remains highly unknown.

Aspartame is an an artificial sweetner that is popular in the US. While generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the FDA, the subject of this n-of-1 did not consume normal amounts. The subject developed an autistic fixation regarding sugar-free gum consumption during a critical window of socioemotional and endocrine development (age 13-14). This sugar free gum was consumed  excessively on an empty stomach due to a year-long eating disorder, making the absorption frequency higher than if it had been consumed with a balanced diet. Furthermore, this individual had a fixation with swallowing the gum due to autistic repetitive behaviors, meaning a larger surface area of these compounds was absorbed.

### Aspartame and BHT Topological, Epigenetic, and Neuroendocrine Impact
Aspartame has been shown in studies to cross-link proteins. This happens because it is metabolized into formaldehyde, which is a known cross-linker of proteins [1](#ref1). I like to think of formaldehyde, the simplest aldehyde, as a topological molecular stapler. It is hypothesized that this cross-linking played a role in both the neurological decline and the formation of the nodules. BHT has also been found to cause thyroid damage in lab studies, and aspartame has been shown to contribute to the development of chronic lymphocytic thyroditis in animal models [citation needed, will add later], but note these are preliminary studies and may not apply to humans. It is also proposed that these compounds and their metabolites (e.g., formaldehyde, methanol) alter epigenomic markers (e.g., potentially upregulating the *NRAS* gene, which one nodule was found to have a mutation in).

Following the consumption of the sugar-free gum with artificial sweeteners and preservatives, the subject experienced a profound negative socioemotional and mental health shift months later. This included the onset of severe depression, insomnia, anxiety, and social withdrawal with unknown etiology. It is hypothesized that the growth of the nodules began concurrently, and as they increased in size, the symptoms became more severe, aligning with the timeline. 

Autistic individuals often have altered metabolic and detoxification pathways. It is hypothesized that the high levels of artificial sweeteners and preservatives in these sugar-free gums combined with sluggish detoxification pathways (e.g., hypothesized *MTHFR* gene mutation based on symptomology, not confirmed) may have contributed to the nodule formation, especially due to the timeline of introducing high levels of this at such a crucial developmental period. Though these compounds are recognized as safe by large regulatory bodies in neurotypical individuals at standard doses, this hypothesis represents how environmental factors should be analyzed more in the etiology of thyroid nodules, and stresses the need for more research into how autistic individuals metabolize compounds differently compared to neurotypicals. Note that this hypothesis is speculative, and the nodules could have developed due to other factors (e.g., autistic differences in thyroids, exposure to volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from living near freeways and automotive manufacturing plants for many years predating puberty), but the profound shift was definitely noted to have occured sharply about three months into the excessive consumption of these sugar-free gums (high-dose aspartame, styrene-like compounds in gum base, and BHT).

### Disclaimer

This serves as a purely exploratory hypothesis, and it has not been proven yet. Aspartame and BHT remain generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by regulatory bodies such as the US FDA at appropriate doses for neurotypical individuals.
